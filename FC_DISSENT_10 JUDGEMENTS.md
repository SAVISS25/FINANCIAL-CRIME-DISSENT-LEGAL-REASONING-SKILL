\## EXTRACTION 4 — SOCRATIC CHALLENGE TO THE MAJORITY

The single most vulnerable assumption in the majority’s reasoning is the premise that \*\*“joint obtaining” of benefit necessarily means each defendant “obtained” the whole benefit for the purposes of POCA 2002 section 76(4), rather than obtained an interest whose value must be separately identified under POCA 2002 section 79(3).\*\*

Why it is vulnerable: It is vulnerable because \*\*POCA’s text repeatedly uses “interest” language (sections 79(3) and 84(2)) and thereby anticipates divided property situations\*\*, so the assumption that “obtained” collapses into “whole obtained by each” is not compelled by the statute and risks turning a proceeds-based measure into an administratively convenient but conceptually punitive duplication, undermining coherence with civil property concepts and Ormerod-style fair labelling.

The Socratic question (one sentence) is: \*\*If POCA 2002 sections 84(2) and 79(3) make confiscation turn on the defendant’s “interest” in property, on what statutory basis does a court treat each joint obtainer as having “obtained” the entire property value rather than the value of his interest?\*\*

The normative trade-off the majority made is that it privileged \*\*enforcement efficiency and deterrent impact\*\* (ensuring the state can recover the full sum from any one of the joint obtainers) while sacrificing \*\*fair labelling/correspondence and civil-criminal doctrinal coherence\*\* (by equating participation in joint obtaining with personal obtaining of the entire value).

How a dissent would use this Socratic challenge to convert it into a counter-ratio: The dissent would translate the question into the proposition that \*\*the statutory scheme itself (especially POCA 2002 section 79(3) read with section 84(2) and section 76(4)) requires an “interest-sensitive” attribution of benefit\*\*, and that courts may not replace that structure with an automatic “whole-benefit replication” rule unless Parliament has clearly authorised that departure; absent such clarity, over-inclusive attribution is an illegitimate shortcut that offends correspondence and risks disproportionate confiscation.

**Whether the Dissent Was Subsequently Vindicated**

Partially vindicated, in two separate respects.

First, the proportionality concern that animated the rejected apportionment argument received substantive doctrinal vindication in _R v Andrewes_ UKSC 24, where the Supreme Court held that a confiscation order made under s 6(5) of the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 can be reduced on A1P1 proportionality grounds where enforcement would produce a disproportionate outcome — specifically where the value obtained by the defendant is substantially offset by a corresponding benefit conferred on the counterparty. Although _Andrewes_ concerned a different factual matrix (fraudulently obtained employment), its reasoning confirmed that the A1P1 proportionality principle operates as a substantive constraint on benefit assessment, not merely a procedural protection at the enforcement stage.\[[macfarlanes](https://www.macfarlanes.com/insights/102l6wv/reap-what-you-sow-proportionality-of-confiscation-orders-assessed-in-cv-fraud-ca)\]​

Second, the Law Commission in its final report, _Confiscation under Part 2 of the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002_ (Law Com No 410, 2022), recommended that the statutory objective of the regime should be placed on a footing of "depriving a defendant of their benefit from criminal conduct, within the limits of their means," explicitly rejecting punishment and deterrence as primary aims. The phrase "within the limits of their means" and the focus on individual benefit is inconsistent with the majority's approach in _Ahmad_, which permits benefit assessment to exceed a defendant's actual receipt without limit. The government accepted the majority of the 119 recommendations in its October 2023 response. No primary legislation has yet been enacted to implement these recommendations as of March 2026, meaning the core apportionment argument has not been legislatively vindicated.lawcom+2

**EXTRACTION 2 — DOCTRINAL GAP IDENTIFICATION**

**Gap the Majority Created**

The majority generated an unresolved ambiguity in the relationship between the first question — has this individual defendant obtained property? — and the second question — what is the value of that property? The court held at para 46 that "it will often be appropriate for a court to hold that each of the conspirators 'obtained' the whole of that property," but immediately qualified this by stating "that will by no means be the correct conclusion in every such case". The gap is the total absence of any principled criterion for identifying the class of cases in which joint obtaining does not lead to full liability for the whole. The court offered only the examples of couriers, drugs mules, and latecomers to a conspiracy (para 47, citing _R v May_ UKHL 28, paras 15 and 17; and _R v Allpress_ EWCA Crim 8; 2 Cr App R (S) 399, paras 80–82), but said nothing about defendants who are principals but who can demonstrate they retained only a portion of the proceeds. **Gap the Majority Ignored**

The majority declined to address the structural disproportionality created when sentencing and confiscation diverge sharply in cases of unequal participation. In the Fields appeal, the trial judge found that Mr Rajput's involvement was "more peripheral" than that of the three Fields defendants, sentenced him to only thirty months' imprisonment, and made only a nominal confiscation order against him because he had established he retained only £12,000. **Live Doctrinal Tension**

The unresolved conflict is between the POCA 2002 regime's insistence that each defendant in a joint criminal enterprise is liable for the full value of the jointly obtained property and the A1P1 proportionality constraint that has progressively expanded following _Waya_ and _Andrewes_. The Ahmad qualification — that orders cannot be enforced twice in relation to the same benefit — addresses disproportionality at the enforcement stage. **Ormerod's Engagement**

David Ormerod addresses the correspondence and fair labelling dimensions of this gap directly. In D Ormerod and K Laird, _Smith, Hogan and Ormerod's Criminal Law_ (15th edn, Oxford University Press, 2018), and in his commentary in the _Criminal Law Review_ on the _May/Green/Jennings_ trilogy, Ormerod identifies the confiscation regime as a site of doctrinal incoherence between criminal law's correspondence principle — which requires that liability be assessed by reference to the harm the individual defendant caused — and the POCA 2002's statutory fiction of full individual liability for collectively obtained proceeds. He has observed that the "severe regime" characterisation in _Waya_ (para 21) is used to justify departure from ordinary criminal law principles, but without explanation of which criminal law principles are displaced and why. The gap identified here falls squarely within all three of Ormerod's intellectual priorities: fair labelling (the "benefit" attributed to each defendant does not accurately describe their individual receipt); correspondence (the quantum of confiscation does not correspond to what the defendant individually obtained); and doctrinal coherence (the civil law treats joint and several liability as subject to contribution rights between tortfeasors, whereas confiscation treats co-conspirators as having no rights inter se while simultaneously imposing joint-enterprise full liability on each).

**EXTRACTION 3 — NEW ARGUMENT DEVELOPMENT**

**Thesis**

The joint and several enforcement qualification introduced in _R v Ahmad and another_ UKSC 36 — which prevents confiscation orders from being enforced to the extent that the same jointly obtained benefit has already been recovered from a co-defendant — is not merely a procedural protection against double recovery but gives substantive content to the A1P1 "fair balance" requirement, and that content requires, as a matter of constitutional law under s 3 of the Human Rights Act 1998, that courts in future cases read s 76(4) of the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 so as to limit the assessed "benefit" of each defendant to the value of property that individual defendant personally assumed dominion over, where such value can be determined with reasonable precision on the evidence — because any excess beyond that value is property neither the defendant has obtained nor any victim has lost, and a confiscation order enforcing that excess bears no rational connection to the statutory objective of depriving defendants of their criminal benefit.

**Gap It Fills**

The existing commentary on _Ahmad_ — including the case note at (2014) UKSC Blog and the practitioner analysis at 15 New Bridge Street Chambers (2014) — treats the joint and several enforcement qualification as a procedural rule preventing double recovery, without engaging with its A1P1 logical foundation. No published scholarship has argued that the Ahmad qualification has substantive constitutional content that reaches back to the benefit assessment stage. This argument enters the gap between (a) the procedural reading of the Ahmad qualification and (b) the expansive A1P1 proportionality principle established in _R v Andrewes_ UKSC 24, where the Supreme Court held that the proportionality proviso in s 6(5) of the 2002 Act operates as a substantive constraint on confiscation at the point of order-making. The contribution is to read Ahmad and Andrewes together as producing a principle that A1P1 proportionality constrains benefit assessment — not merely order enforcement — in cases of joint obtaining where individual shares are ascertainable.nigelpower+2

First, A1P1 requires that any deprivation of possessions must satisfy a "fair balance" between the demands of the public interest and the requirements of the individual's fundamental rights, as the Supreme Court summarised in _R v Waya_ UKSC 51; 1 AC 294, paras 11–13. **Strongest Objection**

The objection at its most powerful is that this argument conflates the confiscation regime's asset-recovery purpose with a proportionality analysis designed to protect property rights, when no defendant has a legitimate expectation in criminal proceeds. The European Court of Human Rights confirmed in _Phillips v United Kingdom_ Crim LR 817 that a statutory assumption that a convicted drug dealer's assets derived from drug trafficking did not infringe A1P1, because "a 'possession' based on unlawful activity is not protected by A1P1" (para 52). Since the defendant has no legitimate expectation in any of the jointly obtained proceeds, A1P1 cannot operate to protect his "interest" in receiving only a proportionate share of confiscation liability at the expense of the state's interest in making full orders against each defendant. Furthermore, Parliament's deliberate choice of a "severe regime" (Waya, para 21) that goes beyond actual receipts reflects a policy judgment that courts should not second-guess.

**Answer to the Objection**

The objection misreads the A1P1 analysis at two levels. At the first level, the _Paulet v United Kingdom_ (Application No 6219/08) (unreported) 13 May 2014 decision, which the court in _Ahmad_ specifically noted at para 77, found a violation of A1P1 in a confiscation context precisely because the order bore no reasonable relationship to the defendant's actual criminal proceeds. The ECHR's A1P1 analysis is not limited to cases where the defendant has a "legitimate expectation" in the property; it extends to cases where the state's interference is disproportionate to its own stated aim, regardless of the nature of the property. At the second level, the "no legitimate expectation" point relates to criminal proceeds themselves; it does not apply to property the defendant never received at all. A confiscation order requiring a defendant to pay £1.6m when he received £533,333 deprives him of £1,066,667 — money that is neither criminal proceeds in his hands nor unrecovered victim loss. That sum is not within the "severe regime" Parliament authorised; it is an artefact of the joint-obtaining legal fiction, and the argument does not use A1P1 to protect criminal proceeds but to constrain the fiction's application beyond the legitimate statutory purpose.\[[ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws](https://ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws.com/web/direct-files/attachments/32956666/df3b4b45-7e6a-4da0-9bd9-fcb190a243a6/R-Appellant-v-Ahmad-and-another-Respondents.md?AWSAccessKeyId=ASIA2F3EMEYE2VNFK6MC&Signature=wTln8PzEMin2NUbpqkjbaD1KclA%3D&x-amz-security-token=IQoJb3JpZ2luX2VjECoaCXVzLWVhc3QtMSJHMEUCIB6JYkGSBn8lErFw5spl3UkVmEVp1%2FiomiJoOeCpw%2FMbAiEA4oWXBpAE8Mf4eVFu4lMsdq79WwSuToamvDCf3XkEhU4q%2FAQI8%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2FARABGgw2OTk3NTMzMDk3MDUiDOJ7ruixqQ%2BM5RMg%2BSrQBETEQzXhTcE8nXMYbjSAuk75PMu8CLCoMDlE4ZXZ5OGn9iPbiHyEgGj2VNCPyHexQl77gmBoyth%2BDJcBesy90Em6j6E5DZo6U7s1Eloodl15NyDZ0o7a0gRDSCSQij6NFw0pDP%2FVdT%2FgGsLic1STcz28oPeWR7AG7PgJkmhap668OEyaPQ1%2BClqsXHyobWZjAOoTTrATr4GivpCUNIc4DpUhQVRwLN8gF%2B%2BJoJeSmTlchoYzfLNZlWaTjyguW6saseM4P8w9j2V4zYXRABTZ9XJ77JJuA%2BVQOXOJFkc8TAdM25m42qAor5Q%2FJTJrYH%2BesLx6nrw3XcfsRmeD96RfKOG0NK6RfGzSVabtfqPNB%2FUtk%2BJY4o2zw2aUFCakb9Lv%2BuVMhz6CG7tZk3gDt6KeiDvgCPrbRdVTuUrjq6M5Ydn3IXUbHHlC7d%2FjjOsbDtrlDaA37wOnEd49gqw31s5TXGaC0jaK4q40CKvnwpyOW69qxmdlqRlxgCZkZ%2FWgC1X46jTJL7hPupxG46wXC0neSky9mQYE%2FoTg0ceFI9MW7XsI35WOqj1ZYy5IBFThUXevCww2NRfu41yB9kjSyWlXigbpk0a6QTaUtb5np%2Fp24JUOyetiHDZqHVR3CfHLkAUSc2z0EvPEyQywonKznf4PHDt4gsYgxFy8rMAzvtmxT1JCVezVAo0qVnhn2UpZ%2B1pPSY016qleUfh%2FwneOyyxlTGHNArtyYkk%2FOpyUdgV8nw%2BeEhsq%2BiuzdOe7OVXnMuib3MO8DP5KZn%2FD6CfW4patb24w9abmzQY6mAHhRK7cMxAE5yygwS3CLkKVqU6fM6PICtJnFyQt4whx%2B9%2Fd6pPP0pxLbV5Ob4RfYwu3H0kxRIVgSOQSZao45JbwDIqk6NMmHekSPNkpzrNzXeq66P1u93ZSah9k23%2BPKEAWWT1vKhX8MAxFSgt%2Bvd6JIpBdSZ2dvqsB%2BBuas0RxyHZKxkEFB69X%2F0Y%2Fx%2BNlKq9I9VV0WceQww%3D%3D&Expires=1773772535)\]​

**Ormerod Test**

Fair labelling: The "benefit" label attributed to each joint obtainer in confiscation proceedings does not accurately describe the culpable conduct it is said to capture. A defendant who received £533,333 in a fraud is labelled as having obtained £1.6m — a label that overstates his culpable receipt by a factor of three and that bears no correspondence to the harm he individually caused. Ormerod's fair labelling criterion requires that the offence label — and by extension the confiscation assessment — accurately describe the conduct. This argument requires that precision. It **passes** the fair labelling test.

Correspondence principle: Ormerod's correspondence principle, as applied to confiscation, requires that the quantum of confiscation liability correspond to what the individual defendant obtained. The Ahmad approach permits benefit assessment to deviate radically from individual receipt, with no correction mechanism beyond the contingent enforcement qualification. This argument reconstructs the correspondence between benefit and individual receipt as a constitutional requirement. It **passes** the correspondence principle test.

Doctrinal coherence: The civil law of joint tortfeasance (a paradigm Ormerod frequently invokes to test coherence) permits full recovery from any one defendant but gives that defendant a contribution claim against co-defendants: _Civil Liability (Contribution) Act 1978_. The confiscation regime imposes the joint liability while denying the contribution right (the Highwayman's Case, _Everet v Williams_ (1725), noted at (1893) 9 LQR 106, invoked at para 63 of _Ahmad_). This argument resolves that incoherence by calibrating confiscation liability to individual receipt in cases where shares are ascertainable, which restores structural symmetry with the civil law. It **passes** the doctrinal coherence test.\[[ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws](https://ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws.com/web/direct-files/attachments/32956666/df3b4b45-7e6a-4da0-9bd9-fcb190a243a6/R-Appellant-v-Ahmad-and-another-Respondents.md?AWSAccessKeyId=ASIA2F3EMEYE2VNFK6MC&Signature=wTln8PzEMin2NUbpqkjbaD1KclA%3D&x-amz-security-token=IQoJb3JpZ2luX2VjECoaCXVzLWVhc3QtMSJHMEUCIB6JYkGSBn8lErFw5spl3UkVmEVp1%2FiomiJoOeCpw%2FMbAiEA4oWXBpAE8Mf4eVFu4lMsdq79WwSuToamvDCf3XkEhU4q%2FAQI8%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2F%2FARABGgw2OTk3NTMzMDk3MDUiDOJ7ruixqQ%2BM5RMg%2BSrQBETEQzXhTcE8nXMYbjSAuk75PMu8CLCoMDlE4ZXZ5OGn9iPbiHyEgGj2VNCPyHexQl77gmBoyth%2BDJcBesy90Em6j6E5DZo6U7s1Eloodl15NyDZ0o7a0gRDSCSQij6NFw0pDP%2FVdT%2FgGsLic1STcz28oPeWR7AG7PgJkmhap668OEyaPQ1%2BClqsXHyobWZjAOoTTrATr4GivpCUNIc4DpUhQVRwLN8gF%2B%2BJoJeSmTlchoYzfLNZlWaTjyguW6saseM4P8w9j2V4zYXRABTZ9XJ77JJuA%2BVQOXOJFkc8TAdM25m42qAor5Q%2FJTJrYH%2BesLx6nrw3XcfsRmeD96RfKOG0NK6RfGzSVabtfqPNB%2FUtk%2BJY4o2zw2aUFCakb9Lv%2BuVMhz6CG7tZk3gDt6KeiDvgCPrbRdVTuUrjq6M5Ydn3IXUbHHlC7d%2FjjOsbDtrlDaA37wOnEd49gqw31s5TXGaC0jaK4q40CKvnwpyOW69qxmdlqRlxgCZkZ%2FWgC1X46jTJL7hPupxG46wXC0neSky9mQYE%2FoTg0ceFI9MW7XsI35WOqj1ZYy5IBFThUXevCww2NRfu41yB9kjSyWlXigbpk0a6QTaUtb5np%2Fp24JUOyetiHDZqHVR3CfHLkAUSc2z0EvPEyQywonKznf4PHDt4gsYgxFy8rMAzvtmxT1JCVezVAo0qVnhn2UpZ%2B1pPSY016qleUfh%2FwneOyyxlTGHNArtyYkk%2FOpyUdgV8nw%2BeEhsq%2BiuzdOe7OVXnMuib3MO8DP5KZn%2FD6CfW4patb24w9abmzQY6mAHhRK7cMxAE5yygwS3CLkKVqU6fM6PICtJnFyQt4whx%2B9%2Fd6pPP0pxLbV5Ob4RfYwu3H0kxRIVgSOQSZao45JbwDIqk6NMmHekSPNkpzrNzXeq66P1u93ZSah9k23%2BPKEAWWT1vKhX8MAxFSgt%2Bvd6JIpBdSZ2dvqsB%2BBuas0RxyHZKxkEFB69X%2F0Y%2Fx%2BNlKq9I9VV0WceQww%3D%3D&Expires=1773772535)\]​

**Why It Is Vulnerable**

The majority's own analysis contains the seed of its refutation. At paras 41–43, the court acknowledged that a criminal who obtains property does not acquire title — only "a possessory interest good against third parties, and thus of no significant value" (citing _R v Waya_ UKSC 51, para 68). The court then stated at para 44 that "obtain" should be given a "broad, normal meaning" and that each conspirator should be treated as having obtained the whole, provided "jointly" is understood in its "ordinary English, and not its technical, legal sense". But the move from the individual statutory word "obtains" to a conclusion of collective total acquisition is not driven by the ordinary meaning of the word; it is driven by prosecutorial policy. The statutory phrase is "he obtains property" — singular, individual. In ordinary English, "he obtained property" does not mean "he participated in a group that collectively obtained property." **Socratic Question**

If "he obtains property" in s 76(4) of the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 means to "assume the rights of an owner" through "a power of disposition or control" (_R v Jennings_ UKHL 29; AC 1046, para 13), on what textual basis does the court attribute to each individual defendant, as property "he" obtained, the full aggregate value of what the criminal enterprise collectively controlled?

**Normative Trade-Off**

The majority privileged the deterrent efficiency and administrative workability of the confiscation regime — specifically the avoidance of investigations into the financial dealings of criminals inter se (para 56) — over the individual defendant's constitutional right to have their liability assessed by reference to their actual criminal receipt. The value sacrificed is doctrinal accuracy: a regime theoretically founded on the proposition that confiscation concerns "proceeds and not profits" (_R v May_ UKHL 28, para 48(3)), and expressly directed at "depriving a defendant of their benefit" (Law Com No 410, 2022), cannot logically assess each defendant's benefit at a figure that may be a multiple of anything they individually received, without abandoning the principle that benefit is an individual — not collective — concept. **How a Dissent Would Use This Socratic Challenge**

A dissenting judge would convert this challenge into a counter-ratio through the following argumentative movement. The starting point is the statutory text: s 76(4) directs the court to determine what "a person" benefits from, and s 7(1) states that the recoverable amount is "the defendant's benefit from the conduct concerned" — both provisions using singular, individualised language. The dissent would argue that the majority's expansion of "obtains" to cover full enterprise-level liability is not a textual conclusion but an interpretive choice driven by deterrence policy, and that Parliament's use of the singular throughout the benefit provisions of the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 is a textual signal that individual acquisition — not collective enterprise participation — is the measure of benefit. The dissent would then apply s 3 of the Human Rights Act 1998, requiring s 76(4) to be read in a manner compatible with A1P1, and would hold — in light of _Paulet v United Kingdom_ (Application No 6219/08) (unreported) 13 May 2014 and the proportionality developments in _R v Andrewes_ UKSC 24 — that in cases where the court can determine the individual defendant's share of the jointly obtained property with reasonable precision, the "fair balance" required by A1P1 demands that assessed benefit be limited to that share. The counter-ratio would therefore be: the statutory objective of depriving each defendant of his individual benefit, read compatibly with A1P1 pursuant to s 3 of the Human Rights Act 1998, requires Thesis (one sentence). The gap it fills. Analytical chain (step-by-step propositions). First, POCA 2002 is proceeds-focused: benefit is “the value of the property obtained” (POCA 2002, s 76(7)) and confiscation is “not an additional fine” (as the judgment states). Fifth, therefore, proportionality demands a doctrinal constraint: the “benefit” attributed to the fraud mechanism must not exceed the defendant’s net retained enrichment causally linked to the deception (eg, interest saved, fees avoided, equity extracted and kept, or appreciation realised and retained), preserving fair labelling (confiscation as proceeds deprivation) and coherence with property/civil reality (repayment restores the lender’s core proprietary position).
